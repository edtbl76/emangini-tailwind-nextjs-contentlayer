I learned how to write before I learned how to present myself.

That probably sounds strange in an era where so much professional advice starts with personal branding, communication strategy, platform building, and polished executive presence. But for many of us who grew up when I did, style came before strategy. Voice came before positioning. You didn't sit down and ask, “What tone should I adopt to optimize my credibility in the market?” You absorbed one by osmosis.

Mine came from a tumultuous cultural weather system: Generation X. It came from Charles Schulz and Bill Watterson. From children and adults who were funny, wounded, observant, awkward, hopeful, and just strange enough to feel real. It came from the bright, rebellious excess of the 1980s, when everything felt louder, more colorful, and less ashamed of its own identity than the preceding generations. It came from the 1990s, which, for all their flaws, still carried a strong current of individualism. There was an assumption that a person ought to sound like a person. That your quirks were not defects in your presentation. They were part of your presence.

That environment was good to me; I don’t know if it was good for me. As a student, and later early in my career, authenticity opened doors. So did empathy. So did humor. So did visible passion. Those things did not replace competence. They amplified it. They made other people lean in. They made conversations more human. They made ideas more memorable. If you had something to say and you could say it with energy, clarity, and a little soul, people responded.

That mattered to me because I was never built from only one thing. My creativity came from music. My intensity came from fighting sports. My curiosity came from technical work. My writing, at its best, was never just about words. It was the place where all of those parts met. It let me think in public in a way that sounded like me. Not a market segment. Not a role. Not a template. Me.

For a while, that was an advantage. Then I got older.

As I moved further into leadership, I started hearing a different message. The same voice that once read as authentic increasingly read as less executive. Humor was riskier. Passion could look like volatility. Strong style could read as undisciplined. If you wanted to move up, I was told, calm would serve you better. Restraint would serve you better. Executive presence would serve you better. I was even told that quirks only endure for the rich and famous.

To be clear, some (most) of that advice was valid. There are real differences between expressing yourself and leading at scale. Senior roles require composure. They require judgment. They require the ability to speak with precision under pressure. They require a kind of emotional regulation that cannot depend on mood, chemistry, or inspiration. So I did not dismiss the feedback. I invested in it.

I pursued business education to supplement technical education. I moved into consulting, in part because it gave me a broader arena in which to observe how senior leaders carried themselves, framed arguments, and built credibility. I hired coaches. I practiced. I revised. I learned how to tighten a point, reduce emotional leakage, remove unnecessary flourish, and sound more measured without becoming hollow. I learned how to project confidence in quieter ways. I learned how to write with a steadier hand.

In other words, I did the work. I found a way to say more with less.

I spent years developing a voice that could still carry conviction while sounding more controlled, more mature, more executive. That development was not cosmetic. It was not just vocabulary and sentence rhythm. It required restraint. Tradeoffs. Self-observation. Feedback. Failure. It required becoming the kind of person who could reliably produce that tone, because it reflected actual judgment.

And then, just as I had gotten good at it, the market changed. Not gradually. Structurally. AI arrived and commoditized the result.

Now, anyone can generate clean, articulate, measured, emotionally moderated, plausibly intelligent prose in seconds. Blog posts, LinkedIn updates, essays, summaries, leadership notes, strategy decks, talking points, website copy, product positioning, investor-friendly language, value statements, think pieces. The supply is effectively infinite. The cost is effectively trivial. The voice that once took years to cultivate can now be approximated on demand.

That doesn't mean AI can truly replicate a person. But it does mean it can replicate enough of the surface area to alter the economics of writing.

And that, I believe, is where the real issue begins. A great deal of public discussion about AI writing has focused on quality. Is it accurate? Is it original? Is it boring? Is it good enough? Those are fair questions, but I don’t think they get to the deepest concern. The more interesting issue is not whether AI writing is bad. Often it is not. The more interesting issue is what happens when a certain kind of polished, socially acceptable, professionally legible voice becomes cheap.

What exactly has been commoditized?

At one level, the answer feels obvious: output. AI helps people produce more written material, faster. But I think something more subtle is happening. What is being commoditized is not just writing volume. It is the appearance of formation. It is the appearance of maturity. It is the appearance of having done the work required to sound measured, organized, and credible.

For a long time, voice carried traces of biography. You could hear where someone came from. You could hear what they had read, what they had survived, what they cared about, what kinds of rooms they had been in, what kinds of failures had refined them. Even when the writing was highly polished, there was often something in it that suggested an actual interior life had shaped it. Voice wasn't merely a presentation layer. It was residue. A pattern left behind by a mind over time.

AI changes that. It doesn't need a childhood. It doesn't need embarrassment. It doesn't need years of trying too hard, sounding wrong, overexplaining, learning to self-edit, losing arguments, getting humbled, finding rhythm, discovering what not to say, and realizing that clarity often costs vanity. It doesn't need to live through enough reality for seriousness to replace performance. It can simply produce the textual markers associated with those things.

That is why so much AI writing feels familiar even when it doesn't feel personal. It has learned the signals. The cadence of balance. The language of reflection. The gentle qualification of strong claims. The respectable rhythm of contemporary seriousness. It can generate the texture of credibility without having traveled the road that once produced credibility. It gives you proximity, but less intimacy.

I don't think we fully understand what that does to readers.

For one thing, it changes our expectations. Once a certain level of polish becomes abundant, readers quickly normalize it. The baseline rises, but strangely. Not because more people became better writers through practice, but because more people gained access to machine-generated approximations. Suddenly, clarity, structure, and tone are no longer scarce signs of effort. They are defaults.

That might sound like progress. In some ways, it is. There is real value in lowering barriers. There is real value in helping people express ideas more clearly. There is real value in making communication less intimidating. I don’t romanticize obscurity, and I don’t think awkwardness is inherently noble, even when it is charming and a characteristic summarization of one's persona. Many people who have important things to say will benefit from tools that help them say them.

But markets don’t merely expand. They also flatten. When a capability becomes easy to reproduce, the market tends to stop paying a premium for it. The differentiator weakens. Scarcity disappears. And once scarcity disappears, perception changes. Something that used to signal development becomes just another expected feature.

That is what I keep circling back to: the cost of not knowing what was earned.

What happens when readers admire a voice that no one had to build? What happens when the traits that used to suggest patience, self-command, reflection, and communication skills can be generated in bulk? What happens when people consume polished, humane-sounding writing without having any way to distinguish whether they are encountering a formed person or a well-aimed machine pattern?

Maybe the answer is that it doesn’t matter. Maybe outcomes are all that matter. Maybe if the text is useful, the origin is irrelevant. That is one plausible future, and perhaps the dominant one.

I'm not convinced it is a harmless shift, because writing has never been only informational. It has always been relational. Even in professional settings, and especially in professional settings, people don't just respond to propositions. They respond to the implied person behind the propositions. To the temperament in the phrasing. To the discipline in the ordering. To the confidence or humility in the framing. To the sense that a mind is present.

If AI can mass-produce the signs of that presence, then we may begin rewarding simulations of judgment in the same way we once rewarded evidence of judgment.

That is not a small distinction.

The issue becomes even more significant when you look at the particular style AI tends to favor. AI is exceptionally good at the center of the distribution. It produces writing that is coherent, moderate, organized, fluent, and safe. It is good at sounding balanced. Good at sounding informed. Good at sounding helpful. Good at sounding executive. Good at sounding thoughtful enough. Not profound. Not deeply original. But plausible, smooth, and frictionless. It has become a pocket executive.

That smoothness has consequences. Some of the most human qualities in writing are jagged. Excessive, even. Idiosyncratic humor. Unfashionable intensity. Peculiar phrasing. Sharp edges. A strange metaphor that only that person would have used. The sentence runs longer than it should because the writer is trying to carry not just an idea but a pulse or idea that is too precious to abandon. The occasional roughness that proves no optimization pass sanded the thing down. It bites, punctures, and leaves impressions behind with a rich flavor that tastes of human condition and a life lived. Of what? Something more than the information that summarizes it. That residue I mentioned before, almost like a fingerprint or the sweat on a cold glass of water.

AI tends to remove those traces. Not because it is malicious, but rather the median is what it knows how to scale. The skew of communication is normalized away to a consistent bell curve. The tone of human errata is flattened into a drone of centrality.

So the risk isn't merely that writing becomes commoditized. It is that we develop a taste for synthetic temperament. That we begin to prefer communication that is permanently moderated, permanently polished, permanently stripped of the inconvenient textures that made people memorable in the first place.

If that happens, the damage will not first emerge as a collapse in usefulness. In fact, usefulness may rise for a while. The damage will show up as sameness. As forgettability. As a thinning out of the public voice. As a world in which more and more people can produce acceptable writing, while fewer and fewer people sound unmistakably alive on the page.

That possibility matters to me because I can feel the collision in my own life.

I came from a generation that taught me to value individuality, then entered a professional culture that taught me to whittle that individuality into something more controlled, and then arrived at a technological moment that industrialized the controlled version. I spent years learning to do by formation what machines can now do by synthesis. That does not make the effort worthless. But it does make me wonder how many people now appreciate the result without recognizing what it took to attain it.

There is a difference between writing calmly and becoming calm enough to write that way.

There is a difference between sounding thoughtful and having lived in a way that produces thoughtfulness.

There is a difference between generating presence and developing presence.

The problem isn't that AI erases those differences internally. The problem is that it may erase them socially. It may make them harder to perceive, harder to value, and therefore less likely to be cultivated by actual people.

That may sound abstract, but its implications are practical. If the market can get polished language cheaply, it may stop caring whether communicators developed an actual voice. If companies can fill channels with coherent articles, respectable posts, and neatly framed points, they may stop rewarding the people who know how to arrive at those points through real observation and struggle. If everyone can sound strategic, then strategic sound may no longer be evidence of strategic thought.

And if that is true, writing is only the first place we will notice it.

Writing makes the problem visible because voice is so explicit on the page. But the underlying issue is larger. It is about what happens when systems can reproduce the outputs associated with judgment before people have actually built the inner machinery of judgment itself.

That pattern does not stop at blogs and LinkedIn posts. It shows up in presentations. In leadership memos. In product narratives. In performance reviews. In strategy documents. In architecture decisions. In design rationale and technical explanations. In every place where language has historically served as a visible proxy for thought.

The same basic dynamic appears again and again.

Something once had to be learned.
Now it can be simulated.

Something once reflected formation.
Now it can be formatted.

Something once suggested character.
Now it can be prompted.

At first, that mostly feels efficient. Then, over time, it changes what institutions reward. That is where I think the conversation becomes much more important than “AI can write blog posts now,” because the deeper issue isn't writing. The deeper issue is whether we are drifting toward a world that values the output signature of capability more than the human formation that capability once required.

That is why I cannot quite treat this as a narrow publishing issue. It is not just about content. It is about authorship. Not in the legal sense. In the human sense. It is about the relationship between what comes out of us and what had to happen inside us in order for that output to exist.

Writing simply gives us the clearest early example.

However, if we dig a little deeper, the most common AI-generated content isn’t the written word. It’s written code. And by a wide margin.

Code is often treated as purely technical, and, of course, it is technical. But it is also expressive. Not expressive in the same way as prose, but expressive nonetheless. Every system carries traces of temperament. What is made obvious and what is hidden. What is overengineered, and what is left simple. What gets tested. What gets documented. What gets abstracted. What gets deferred. What gets tolerated. What gets optimized. What gets made legible for the next person. These are not just implementation details. They are choices. They reflect values, tradeoffs, priorities, patience, restraint, fear, vanity, generosity, courage, and judgment.

In other words, code also reveals character. (And, no, I’m not referring to ASCII or Unicode encoding). Not moral character in some grandiose sense, either, but professional character. Human character under constraint. The shape of a mind making decisions in a real environment with real consequences.

For a long time, that mattered because there was no shortcut around it. To write good code consistently, you had to become someone capable of making those tradeoffs well. You had to learn where cleverness hurts. Where simplicity wins. Where speed creates debt. Where abstraction helps and where it lies. You had to earn your taste. You had to earn your judgment. You had to be formed by failure, maintenance, scale, collaboration, outages, regret, and responsibility.

What we are seeing AI do for prose, it has already been doing for code.

It can generate the shape of competence. It can produce plausible structure. It can imitate patterns associated with clean implementation. It can approximate the textual and structural signatures of judgment. Sometimes it can do this remarkably well. Sometimes well enough to be useful. Sometimes, well enough to impress people who do not yet know what they are looking at.

But the same questions remain:

What happens when the output of judgment becomes easier to reproduce than the formation that judgment requires?

What happens when people can generate code that resembles good decisions without having developed the instincts that help them recognize where the code is fragile, overfit, leaky, misleading, or quietly dangerous?

What happens when the market starts rewarding the appearance of fluency over the development of taste?

This is why I find the discussion around AI and writing so revealing. Writing is not just a side effect. It is the canary.

In writing, we can already see what it looks like when a machine makes competence abundant and character ambiguous. We can already see what happens when polish becomes cheap, when voice drifts toward a common center, and when readers lose sight of what used to separate developed expression from generated approximation.

If we cannot think clearly about that in writing, we are not going to think clearly about it in code.

Because in both cases, the danger is not simply that AI can produce output. The danger is that we may begin to forget why the human process behind the output mattered in the first place.

The danger is that we will confuse articulation with understanding. We will confuse smoothness with maturity. We will confuse generated presence with developed character.
We will confuse code that compiles with judgment that can be trusted.

Maybe some of this is just the normal pain of transition. Maybe the culture will adapt. Maybe people will become better at detecting what is alive and what is merely polished. Maybe authenticity will reprice itself in a new way. I hope so. Human beings have a long history of finding new signals when old ones collapse.

I don’t think we should dismiss the collapse just because a new equilibrium may eventually emerge. Something meaningful is changing right now.

A voice that once had to be built can now be borrowed.
A style that once reflected formation can now be generated.
A presence that once suggested a person can now be simulated.

And if that only affected writing, it would already be worth taking seriously. But it does not stop there, because character shows up in more than just writing. Character shows up anywhere judgment leaves a trace.

It shows up in how we explain.
It shows up in how we decide.
It shows up in what we optimize.
It shows up in what we leave behind for the next person.
It shows up in how much of ourselves we had to become before the work could look the way it does.

That is true in essays.
That is true in leadership.
That is true in architecture.
And yes, that is true in code.

So the question I keep coming back to is not whether AI can write like us.

It is whether we are entering a world that no longer cares whether anyone became the kind of person who could have written it.

To be clear, I'm not against AI. I make a living creating it, working with it, and feverishly finding ways to be equipped by it.

I very much believe that AI affords us the opportunity to do better and to be better. To do that, I very much believe we must carry our voices with us at all times. To that end, I’ll leave you with a quote from a movie that inspired me to carry mine:

Talk Hard.
